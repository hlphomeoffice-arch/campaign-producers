AI, TRADITIONAL OR HYBRID? START WITH THE CAMPAIGN JOB.

The most expensive production mistake is choosing the method before deciding what the campaign must make people notice, feel or do.

By Henk PretoriusFounder / Campaign Producer

Marketing teams are being asked a seductive question: should this campaign be made with AI? It sounds like a production question, but it arrives too early. The first question is simpler and far more important: what must this campaign achieve in the mind and behaviour of the audience?

Until that is clear, AI can make the wrong idea cheaper. Traditional production can make it more beautiful. A hybrid workflow can make it more complicated. None of those outcomes creates value by itself.

THE PRODUCTION METHOD IS NOT THE STRATEGY

A campaign needs to move a person from one state to another. They may need to notice a problem, understand an unfamiliar offer, believe a promise, feel urgency or take a specific action. That movement is the commercial job.

Once the job is defined, each asset can be judged against it. The hero film may need to create emotion. A landing page may need to remove doubt. Short social pieces may need to make one idea memorable. The production method should then be chosen according to what each asset needs to accomplish.

Choose the campaign job first. Choose the production route second.

This order matters because production tools shape the work. If a team begins with a tool, the campaign often bends around what that tool can make. If the team begins with the audience, the tool has to earn its place.

WHERE AI EARNS ITS PLACE

AI production is powerful when the campaign benefits from speed, visual invention, rapid variation or an idea that would be disproportionately expensive to create physically. It can help a smaller budget express a larger imaginative world. It can also help a team explore possibilities before committing money to final execution.

It is particularly useful when:

  • The idea depends on transformation, impossible scale or a highly stylised visual world.
  • The campaign needs many deliberate versions for different audiences, formats or markets.
  • Previsualisation would help decision-makers approve the idea before full production.
  • The work can be built from controlled assets without relying on uncontrolled likeness, copyright or product-detail risks.
  • Speed matters and the team has the experience to direct, review and finish AI output properly.

AI is not a button that removes production. It changes where production effort lives. The work moves into asset preparation, prompting, iteration, compositing, continuity, retouching, sound and quality control. A cheap generation can still require expensive judgement.

WHERE TRADITIONAL PRODUCTION STILL LEADS

Traditional production remains difficult to beat when trust depends on reality. A real performance, a genuine customer, a physical product, a recognisable location or a moment of human imperfection can carry meaning that a synthetic image does not automatically create.

It is often the stronger route when:

  • The audience needs to believe that the person, place, product or demonstration is real.
  • Performance is the emotional centre of the campaign.
  • Product details must be completely accurate from every angle.
  • The brand needs documentary evidence, testimony or a credible human encounter.
  • The captured material will become a long-term library across many future campaigns.

Traditional production can look more expensive on the first estimate while creating more reusable value. One well-planned shoot can produce the hero film, photography, interviews, behind-the-scenes material and a bank of authentic brand assets. The right comparison is not the cost of one image. It is the value and useful life of the complete asset system.

WHY HYBRID OFTEN WINS

The choice is rarely ideological. A strong campaign does not need to prove loyalty to AI or traditional craft. It needs to create the best result within the available time, budget and risk.

A hybrid route can protect the parts that must be real and use AI where it adds reach or imagination. You might photograph the people and product accurately, then use AI to build an impossible environment. You might film a performance, extend the world digitally and create format variations without repeating the full shoot. You might use generated previsualisation to plan a short, precise production day.

The value comes from assigning each method a clear job. Hybrid becomes wasteful when it is used as a vague promise that anything can be fixed later.

A FIVE-QUESTION DECISION TEST

Before approving a production route, ask these questions in order:

  1. What must the audience understand, feel or do after seeing this?
  2. Which part of the idea must be real for the promise to be believed?
  3. Which part would gain genuine value from invention, scale or variation?
  4. What assets must exist beyond the hero piece, and how long must they remain useful?
  5. Which route gives us the strongest result after quality control, rights, finishing and revisions are included?

If those answers point to AI, use it confidently. If they point to a camera, crew and real performance, protect that choice. If they point to a deliberate combination, design the handover between methods before production begins.

SPEND WHERE THE AUDIENCE CAN FEEL IT

A production budget should not be spread evenly. It should concentrate on the moments that create belief, emotion and action. The audience does not reward a campaign because every part was expensive. They respond when the right part feels undeniable.

That may mean spending on a remarkable performer and simplifying the setting. It may mean filming the product carefully and generating the surrounding world. It may mean using AI for a striking hero image while investing human time in the message, edit, sound and final call to action.

The smartest production route is not the newest or the most traditional. It is the one that helps the campaign say what matters and spend where it counts.
